How do seismologists use diagrams to determine which seismograph is located farthest from the earthquake?

(A) The amplitude of the Primary Waves

(B) The amplitude of the Secondary Waves

(C) The smallest difference in arrival time of the P and S waves

(D) ** The largest difference in arrival time of the P and S waves

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -The longer the time is between the P-and S-wave arrivals, the further away the earthquake was from the seismograph. First, seismologists calculate the arrival time difference. Then they know the distance to the epicenter from that seismograph.

Concept note-2: -By looking at the seismograms from different recording stations, we can find out the epicentre of the earthquake. The signals arrive first at the closest station and last at the one furthest away. The time difference between the P-and S-waves tells us the distance the earthquake is from the seismometer.

Concept note-3: -Each seismograph records the times when the first (P waves) and second (S waves) seismic waves arrive. From that information, scientists can determine how fast the waves are traveling. Knowing this helps them calculate the distance from the epicenter to each seismograph.

Concept note-4: -The simplest method of locating an earthquake on a globe is to find the time interval between the P-and S-wave arrivals at several seismograph stations. The distance to the earthquake from each station is then determined from standard travel-time tables and travel-time curves.

Concept note-5: -Answer and Explanation: Seismologists align the seismograph with the time-distance graph by aligning the first appearance of the S-wave with the S-wave curve on the time-distance graph.
