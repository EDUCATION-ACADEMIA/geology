Bonus question:Where does an ocean currents become more dence?

(A) ** in polar regions

(B) in temperate regions

(C) near continents

(D) near the equator

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -Deep ocean currents are density-driven and differ from surface currents in scale, speed, and energy. Water density is affected by the temperature, salinity (saltiness), and depth of the water. The colder and saltier the ocean water, the denser it is.

Concept note-2: -In the Earthâ€™s polar regions ocean water gets very cold, forming sea ice. As a consequence the surrounding seawater gets saltier, because when sea ice forms, the salt is left behind. As the seawater gets saltier, its density increases, and it starts to sink.

Concept note-3: -Deep ocean currents In cold regions, such as the North Atlantic Ocean, ocean water loses heat to the atmosphere and becomes cold and dense. When ocean water freezes, forming sea ice, salt is left behind causing surrounding seawater to become saltier and denser. Dense-cold-salty water sinks to the ocean bottom.

Concept note-4: -The warm ocean currents move from equator to poles and the cold ocean currents move from poles towards equators.
