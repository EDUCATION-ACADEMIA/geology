Why do plates sometimes sink into the mantle?

(A) because of convergent boundaries

(B) Because they feel like it

(C) Because one is more cool so it sinks first

(D) ** Subduction one plate is more dense

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -The overridden plate (the slab) sinks at an angle of approximately 25 to 75 degrees to Earthâ€™s surface. This sinking is driven by the temperature difference between the slab and the surrounding asthenosphere, as the colder oceanic lithosphere has, on average, a greater density.

Concept note-2: -Where two tectonic plates converge, if one or both of the plates is oceanic lithosphere, a subduction zone will form. An oceanic plate will sink back into the mantle. Remember, oceanic plates are formed from mantle material at midocean ridges.

Concept note-3: -When these collisions occur, the more dense plate slides below the other, which is a process called subduction. So for oceanic-oceanic boundaries, the more dense oceanic plate sinks below the less dense oceanic plate.

Concept note-4: -As a result, low density owing to hydration reduces subduction rates, back-arc spreading, and slab stagnation on the phase boundary at 660-km depth, whereas low viscosity owing to hydration enhances rapid subduction, trench migration, and slab stagnation.

Concept note-5: -When an oceanic plate converges with a continental plate, the oceanic crust will always subduct under the continental crust; this is because oceanic crust is naturally denser.
