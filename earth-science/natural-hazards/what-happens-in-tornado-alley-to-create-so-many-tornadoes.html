What happens in Tornado Alley to create so many tornadoes?

(A) The rate of condensation is higher than the rate of precipitation

(B) Warm air from Canada meets cold air from Virginia

(C) ** Cold, dry air from Canada meets warm dry air from Gulf of Mexico

(D) Warm air in Texas clashes with even warmer air in Texas

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -In this area, known as Tornado Alley, storms are caused when dry cold air moving south from Canada meets warm moist air traveling north from the Gulf of Mexico. Tornadoes can form at any time of year, but most occur in the spring and summer months along with thunderstorms.

Concept note-2: -In Tornado Alley, warm, humid air from the equator meets cool to cold, dry air from Canada and the Rocky Mountains. This creates an ideal environment for tornadoes to form within developed thunderstorms and supercells.

Concept note-3: -Tornadoes form from strong thunderstorms. In Canada, conditions that can create a tornado involve warm, moist air travelling up from the U.S., which then combines with cool air from am existing storm. As the airs mix, they create an instability and an updraft, which is strong winds pulling toward the clouds.

Concept note-4: -In Canada, tornadoes most commonly occur in southernmost Saskatchewan, Manitoba and Ontario, but can also occur in southern Alberta, southern Quebec and New Brunswick. NTP data suggests peak tornado season in southern Ontario is now more likely later in the summer.

Concept note-5: -The key atmospheric ingredients that lead to tornado potential are instability-warm moist air near the ground, with cooler dry air aloft and wind shear-a change in wind speed and/or direction with height.
